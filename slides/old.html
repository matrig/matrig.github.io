NeurIPS: From Controversy to Progress

The Name Change Story (2018)

  • Founded 1987 as ‚ÄúNIPS (Neural Information Processing Systems)‚Äù24
  • Community concerns for name appropriateness with shifts toward inclusivity
    • Name lent itself to sexualized puns ‚Üí hostile environment for women
    • Slur against Japanese people
  • 2017: Resistance and escalation
    • Ultra-libertarians doubled down defending the name
    • Elon Musk‚Äôs crude jokes on stage at the conference defending free speech absolutism, events named ‚ÄúTITS‚Äù, lewd t-shirts25
    • Escalated the crisis - made name untenable

The Grassroots Movement:

  • March 2018: Johns Hopkins petition for name change
  • April 2018: Board begins collecting community feedback
  • October 16, 2018: Board announces ‚Äúkeeping NIPS‚Äù
  • October-November: #ProtestNIPS backlash erupts
    • Led by Anima Anandkumar (CalTech, NVIDIA)
    • 2,000+ signatures, boycott threats
    • Peter Henderson (Stanford PhD) purchases neurips.cc domain
  • November 16, 2018: Emergency reversal ‚Üí NeurIPS

Lesson from NeurIPS Name Change

Institutions Evolve Through Collective Will

Established institutions carry weight and legitimacy for good reason. NeurIPS, founded in 1987, had 31 years of crystallized tradition and identity.

Yet institutions derive their legitimacy from the communities that sustain them, and must remain accountable to those communities as they evolve.

The Mechanism of Institutional Change:

  • Led by institutional insiders (e.g. Anima Anandkumar)
  • Enabled by grassroots mobilization (community signatures, coordinated pressure)
  • Catalyzed by individual agency (Peter Henderson, Stanford PhD student, secured neurips.cc domain and enacted the alternative, making it tangible)
  • Not negotiable when aligned: institutional accountability + reputational risk + viable alternative = institutional reversal

The change succeeded because it married leadership capacity with grassroots momentum.

The Hard Lesson: Institutional change requires perpetual vigilance; progress is ground that must be continuously defended

Progress Faces Continued Resistance

Institutional gatekeepers repackaged reactionary opposition as reasoned principle, delegitimizing change after it succeeded.26

ICLR: The Transparent One

Founded 2012 by Yann LeCun & Yoshua Bengio27

  • When deep learning was still controversial, founders created a dedicated venue
  • First ICLR (May 2013): filled a void for representation learning researchers
  • ICLR grew with the field‚Äôs rise.

The Innovation: Radical Transparency

In 2013, Andrew McCallum‚Äôs lab at UMass Amherst created OpenReview, a platform to ‚Äúpromote openness in scientific communication‚Äù through transparent peer review.28 ICLR became its flagship adopter.

The entire peer review process is made public:

  • Reviews visible in real-time
  • Authors and reviewers engage publicly
  • Review process becomes scientific record

Strategic Positioning

  • Deep learning specialization (emerged with the field)
  • Higher acceptance (~32%) ‚Üí room for exploratory ideas
  • September timing ‚Üí iterate after NeurIPS/ICML

The Trade-off: Transparency‚Äôs Cost

Opportunity: Peer accountability; bad reviews are exposed; unjust criticism can be rebutted publicly

Risk: Your entire review history‚Äîpositive and negative‚Äîis permanently public. No anonymity from community judgment.

Democratizing review means dissolving review anonymity.

Practical Tips

Choosing Your Venue

Ask yourself:

  1. Audience? General ML vs CV/NLP
  2. Theory vs applications? ICML vs NeurIPS vs ICLR
  3. Maturity? Workshop vs main conference
  4. Timeline? Check deadlines!

Conference Submission Cycle:

ICLR (Sept) ‚Üí ICML (Jan) ‚Üí NeurIPS (May) ‚Üí Repeat

Smart strategy: Rejection from ICLR (Dec) ‚Üí iterate for ICML (Jan) ‚Üí or NeurIPS (May)

The Rejection Reality Check

The Numbers:

  • 73-76% rejection rate at top venues35
  • First submission rejection is NORMAL
  • It‚Äôs not personal

What to do:

  1. Wait 24-48 hours before reading reviews
  2. Identify valid criticisms (ignore unfair ones)
  3. Improve the paper based on feedback
  4. Resubmit to the next venue
